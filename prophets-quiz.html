God's Messengers Through the Ages

Prophets were God's spokespeople—men and women He chose to deliver His messages to Israel and the nations. From Isaiah's visions to Jonah's reluctant mission, these prophets shaped Israel's history and pointed forward to Christ.

What Prophets Actually Did

Prophets weren't primarily fortune-tellers. Yes, they predicted future events—especially about the coming Messiah—but that was only part of their job. Mostly, prophets called Israel to repent from idolatry, warned of coming judgment if they didn't turn back to God, and reminded them of God's covenant promises. They comforted the afflicted and afflicted the comfortable.

Isaiah prophesied during the reigns of four kings of Judah. He warned of Assyrian and Babylonian invasions, but also predicted a suffering servant who would bear the sins of many—a clear prophecy about Jesus. Jeremiah watched Jerusalem fall to Babylon despite warning them for decades. Ezekiel had bizarre visions while in Babylonian exile. Daniel interpreted dreams and saw visions of world empires rising and falling.

The minor prophets aren't called "minor" because they're less important—they're just shorter books. Hosea's marriage to an unfaithful wife illustrated God's relationship with Israel. Amos, a shepherd, condemned Israel's social injustice. Jonah ran from God's call to preach to Nineveh. Each prophet had a specific message for a specific time, but their words still speak today.

Key Prophets to Know

Isaiah: Prophesied about the virgin birth, the suffering servant, and the coming kingdom. His book is quoted more in the New Testament than any other prophet. Isaiah 53 describes Jesus' crucifixion 700 years before it happened.

Jeremiah: Called "the weeping prophet" because he wept over Jerusalem's coming destruction. He bought a field during the siege to show Israel would return. He predicted the new covenant that Jesus would establish.

Ezekiel: Saw visions of God's glory leaving the temple, a valley of dry bones coming to life, and a future temple. He acted out prophecies—lying on his side for 390 days, shaving his head, packing bags for exile.

Daniel: Interpreted King Nebuchadnezzar's dreams, survived the lions' den, and saw visions of the Messiah's coming. His prophecies give specific timelines that many believe predicted Jesus' arrival.

Jonah: Ran from God's call to preach to Nineveh, got swallowed by a great fish, finally obeyed, and got angry when the Ninevites repented. Jesus referenced Jonah as a sign of His own death and resurrection.

Questions About This Prophets Quiz

Do I need to read all the prophets? For this old testament prophets quiz, focus on Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel, and Jonah. Read at least one chapter from each of the twelve minor prophets to get familiar with their names and basic messages.

Why are prophets hard to understand? They often used symbolic language, addressed situations you might not know about, and spoke to people who lived 2,500+ years ago. Reading the historical books (Kings, Chronicles) alongside the prophets helps you understand the context.

How will this help my Bible study? Understanding the prophets helps you see God's heart for justice, His patience with sinful people, and His plan for redemption. Many prophecies point to Jesus, so knowing the prophets deepens your appreciation for how the whole Bible fits together.
