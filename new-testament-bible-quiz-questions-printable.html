Why the New Testament Deserves Deeper Study Than Most Classes Give It

Most church members can tell you that Jesus was born in Bethlehem, died on the Cross, and rose again on the third day. Far fewer can tell you who Apollos was, what the Bereans were commended for, what Paul was doing when he wrote Philippians, or what the seven churches of Revelation actually represented. The New Testament is far deeper than its best-known verses suggest.

The Epistles alone — Romans through Jude — contain some of the most theologically dense writing in human history. Paul's letter to the Romans is a systematic theology of salvation that has shaped Christian doctrine for two thousand years. Yet most Sunday School curricula treat the Epistles as a source for memory verses rather than a body of teaching to be studied and tested.

Quiz-based learning works for the New Testament because it forces engagement with the text at a granular level. A student who correctly answers "In which city did Paul leave Trophimus sick?" (Miletum — 2 Timothy 4:20) has read that passage. The wrong answer is the teacher's opportunity. The right answer is proof that the Word has taken root.
